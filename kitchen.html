Bourbon in food, not just in glasses. Recipes and pairings that actually work.

Bourbon is a cooking ingredient that most people underutilize. It's not just for glazes — the caramel and vanilla notes work in sauces, the barrel char provides smokiness, and the alcohol carries fat-soluble flavors in ways wine and vinegar can't. This section is about using it properly.

Recipes

Dishes with bourbon as a real ingredient, not a gimmick.

Bourbon Brown Sugar Salmon

Dinner · 30 min · Serves 4

A simple glaze — Elijah Craig, brown sugar, soy, garlic, and a little heat — that caramelizes beautifully on salmon. One of the highest-effort-to-result-ratio meals I make. The bourbon cuts through the richness and the char from the pan mirrors what's happening in the barrel.

Bourbon Peach BBQ Sauce

Condiment · 45 min · Makes 2 cups

The house BBQ sauce. Built around fresh peaches (or canned in winter), Wild Turkey 101, and a solid hit of smoked paprika. Goes on ribs, chicken, burgers — and yes, I use it as a dipping sauce for sweet potato fries.

Kentucky Butter Cake

Dessert · 1 hour · Serves 10

This is the one people always ask about. Dense, buttery pound cake with a bourbon butter sauce poured over while hot — it soaks in and creates a slightly crisp exterior. Uses Maker's Mark because you don't need complexity here, you need sweetness and vanilla.

Pairing Notes

What to eat while you're drinking what.

Pairing

High Rye Bourbons

Spice-forward ryes pair well with charcuterie, aged cheese, and anything with a mustard component. The rye spice complements rather than fights the savory notes.

Pairing

Wheated Bourbons

The softer, sweeter profile of wheated bourbons works beautifully with chocolate desserts, pecan-forward dishes, and smoked meats where you want complement, not contrast.

Pairing

Cask Strength

High-proof bourbons need food with enough fat and richness to stand up. Bold cheeses, short ribs, and dark chocolate work. Light salads do not.
